White starts any game with tempo;
that's what makes playing White/Black different.
With 1.a3, one could argue that White has
(mostly) given up their opening tempo,
and indeed many lines from this point can just progress as though
Black had been the one to move first all along.
For instance, consider that 1...e5 2.e4 Nf6 3.Nc3 d5,
from Black's perspective, is essentially just a Scotch where they moved first,
and White has a weird a-pawn.
For White, then, Anderssen's Opening can be considered a useful tool
if they would have rather played as black for some reason.

That's a neat-and-tidy explanation, but it isn't quite that simple.
White has *mostly* given up a tempo, but it's more like a half-tempo.
a3 has increased their control over the b4 square
(and maybe weakened b3, which is rarely relevant).

So, with that in mind, there are really 3 kinds
of continuations after Anderrsen's Opening.

1. a3 wasn't important, and play proceeds as though Black had moved first.
2. a3 *does* end up being important.
  White may be able to enter the occasional Anderssen-specific lines,
  or White gets a favorable version of some Black opening.
  Eg, 1.a3 e5 2.d4 exd4 3.Nc3 is like a Modern Scandinavian,
  but Black doesn't have the option of Bb4+.
3. The opening transposes into something where
  a3 would have been played anyways.
  Eg, 1.e4 c5 2.a3, a Mengarini Sicilian

It's the second case which interests me. ...
